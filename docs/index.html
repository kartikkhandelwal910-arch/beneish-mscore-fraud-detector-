The Model

Eight ratios. One verdict.

Beneish's 1999 model condenses a full set of financial statements into eight indices, each capturing a different way earnings get quietly manipulated — inflated receivables, shrinking margins, capitalized expenses. Combined, they produce a single M-Score.

DSRI
Days' Sales in Receivables Index

Receivables growing faster than sales — a classic sign of channel-stuffing or premature revenue recognition.

×0.920
GMI
Gross Margin Index

Deteriorating margins create pressure to manipulate earnings elsewhere in the statements.

×0.528
AQI
Asset Quality Index

A rising share of soft, non-current assets — where costs get deferred instead of expensed.

×0.404
SGI
Sales Growth Index

Fast growth isn't manipulation on its own, but it's where the pressure to manipulate is highest.

×0.892
DEPI
Depreciation Index

A slowing depreciation rate can mean assets are being written down more slowly to flatter earnings.

×0.115
SGAI
SG&A Index

Disproportionate SG&A growth relative to sales, often a sign of weakening cost discipline.

×0.172
LVGI
Leverage Index

Rising leverage can create incentive to manipulate to stay within covenant thresholds.

×0.327
TATA
Total Accruals to Total Assets

The gap between reported earnings and actual cash flow — the most direct accrual-quality signal.

×4.679

Two Ways to Flag

A fixed threshold, and a fairer one.

Beneish's original −1.78 cutoff was calibrated on a mostly-industrial, pre-2000 US sample. It doesn't transfer cleanly to every sector — so every company is also scored against its own industry peers.

0.00

Beneish's original 1999 cutoff. Scores above this line are flagged under the fixed, published model — useful as a common reference point across any dataset.

Sector-Relative Flag

How unusual a company's score is versus same-SIC-code peers. Catches manipulation the fixed threshold misses in sectors that don't resemble Beneish's original sample.

Architecture

Where the numbers actually come from.

US filers are pulled directly from SEC EDGAR's XBRL data — no third-party financial-data vendor in between. Everything else falls back to Yahoo Finance, and every row is tagged with its source so the two are never mistaken for one dataset.

Step 1
Route the ticker

US SEC filer → EDGAR XBRL. Other exchange → Yahoo Finance. Every row tagged data_source.

Step 2
Pull statements

10–15+ years of US GAAP history from EDGAR; ~4 years of IFRS statements from Yahoo for international names.

Step 3
Compute the score

8-ratio Beneish M-Score, academic and sector-relative flagging, accounting-identity validation on every row.

Step 4
Render the dashboard

Trend line, peer comparison bar, ratio radar, and summary table — one live Streamlit app, no setup required.

Coverage

Built for more than the S&P 500.

US tickers run straight through EDGAR. International names route through Yahoo Finance by exchange suffix — read as directional versus US GAAP filings, not strictly apples-to-apples.

Disclosures

What this model can't do.

A forensic tool that hides its own blind spots isn't a serious one. These are read verbatim off the model's own documentation.

Known Limitations

Not meaningful for banks or insurers. They don't report COGS or a classified balance sheet, so GMI, AQI, and LVGI can't be computed — the app detects this and warns explicitly.
International results use IFRS, not US GAAP. Ratios still compute, but cross-border comparisons should be read as directional, not precise.
Shorter history outside the US. Yahoo's free tier returns ~4 fiscal years versus 15+ from SEC XBRL — fewer year-over-year pairs, noisier ratios.
Data warnings are heuristic. 5% tolerance checks on the accounting identity, not a full double-entry reconciliation.
No formal test suite yet. Validated via synthetic edge cases — zero-revenue years, missing fields, mixed-source pipelines — rather than pytest.
